# 5 Breathing Techniques That Actually Work for Anxiety (Backed by Science)

*By Collin Chartier, Certified Breathwork Instructor & Founder of Ground App*

---

When anxiety hits, your body doesn't need a pep talk. It needs a physiological intervention. These five breathing techniques work because they act directly on your nervous system — not your mindset.

I'm a certified breathwork instructor, and these are the techniques I use with my clients (and myself) when anxiety is acute and real. No fluff. Just the mechanisms and the protocols.

## 1. Physiological Sigh — The Fastest Reset (1 breath cycle)

**The technique:** Double inhale through your nose (a full inhale, then a short "sip" of air on top), followed by a long, slow exhale through your mouth.

**Why it works:** The double inhale maximally inflates the alveoli (tiny air sacs) in your lungs that tend to collapse when you're stressed and breathing shallowly. Reinflating them offloads CO2 more efficiently. The extended exhale then stimulates the vagus nerve and triggers parasympathetic activation.

**The research:** Stanford neuroscientist Dr. Andrew Huberman's lab published research showing the Physiological Sigh is the most effective real-time stress reduction technique — a single breath cycle produces measurable decreases in physiological arousal.

**Protocol:** 3-5 breath cycles. That's it. You can do this in a meeting, in your car, or before walking on stage. Total time: under 60 seconds.

**Best for:** Acute anxiety, panic onset, pre-performance nerves, mid-conversation stress.

## 2. 4-7-8 Breathing — The Anxiety and Sleep Technique

**The technique:** Inhale through your nose for 4 counts. Hold for 7 counts. Exhale through your mouth for 8 counts.

**Why it works:** The exhale-to-inhale ratio (2:1) is the key. An exhale twice as long as the inhale maximizes vagus nerve stimulation and parasympathetic activation. The 7-count hold improves CO2 tolerance, which directly addresses anxiety sensitivity — the "I can't breathe" sensation that feeds panic.

**The research:** Developed by Dr. Andrew Weil based on pranayama breathing principles. Clinical studies show it reduces blood pressure, heart rate, and subjective anxiety scores after just 4 cycles.

**Protocol:** 4 cycles to start. Work up to 8 cycles. Practice twice daily for cumulative nervous system benefits.

**Best for:** Pre-sleep anxiety, generalized anxiety, racing thoughts, difficulty unwinding at night.

## 3. Box Breathing — Composure Under Pressure (4-4-4-4)

**The technique:** Inhale for 4 counts. Hold for 4 counts. Exhale for 4 counts. Hold for 4 counts. Repeat.

**Why it works:** The equal ratios create a balanced autonomic state — not too parasympathetic (which would make you sleepy), not too sympathetic. The breath holds improve CO2 tolerance and activate the prefrontal cortex, restoring executive function that anxiety suppresses. This is why you can think more clearly after Box Breathing.

**The research:** Used by Navy SEALs, law enforcement, and first responders for maintaining composure under acute stress. Studies show Box Breathing reduces cortisol levels and improves cognitive performance under pressure.

**Protocol:** 5 minutes (approximately 5 cycles per minute, 25 cycles total). Can extend to 10-15 minutes for deeper regulation.

**Best for:** High-pressure situations, pre-performance anxiety, needing to stay alert but calm, decision-making under stress.

## 4. Coherent Breathing — HRV Maximizer (5.5 in, 5.5 out)

**The technique:** Inhale for 5.5 seconds. Exhale for 5.5 seconds. No holds. Continuous.

**Why it works:** Breathing at approximately 5.5 breaths per minute (this specific rate) maximizes a phenomenon called respiratory sinus arrhythmia — the natural variation in heart rate that occurs with each breath cycle. This produces the highest possible heart rate variability (HRV), which is the gold standard biomarker for nervous system health and emotional regulation.

**The research:** Documented extensively in Stephen Elliott's research and validated in multiple clinical trials. Regular Coherent Breathing practice increases resting HRV within 4-8 weeks, which translates to measurably better stress resilience.

**Protocol:** 10-20 minutes daily for training effect. 5 minutes for acute regulation. This is the "daily vitamin" of breathwork — consistent practice compounds dramatically.

**Best for:** Daily nervous system maintenance, HRV training, chronic anxiety baseline reduction, long-term stress resilience building.

## 5. Extended Exhale Breathing — The Simplest Calm-Down (4-8 or 3-6)

**The technique:** Inhale for 4 counts. Exhale for 8 counts. Or inhale for 3, exhale for 6. The specific numbers don't matter — what matters is the exhale is twice as long as the inhale.

**Why it works:** This is the most basic parasympathetic trigger. The extended exhale activates the vagus nerve through diaphragmatic pressure and shifts blood gas chemistry toward CO2 retention, which slows heart rate and promotes calm.

**The research:** The most well-studied breathwork ratio in clinical literature. Meta-analyses consistently show extended exhale breathing reduces physiological markers of anxiety (heart rate, cortisol, galvanic skin response) within 2-3 minutes.

**Protocol:** 2-5 minutes. Start with whatever counts feel comfortable — even 2 in, 4 out. The ratio matters more than the absolute numbers.

**Best for:** Beginners, moments when you can't remember other techniques, ongoing regulation throughout the day.
